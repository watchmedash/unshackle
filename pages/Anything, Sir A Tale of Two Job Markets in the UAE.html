Anything, Sir: A Tale of Two Job Markets in the UAE

In the shimmering skyline of the UAE, beneath towers of glass and steel, a curious pattern plays out in HR offices and interview rooms across the Emirates.

I’ve been hiring for years. From Dubai to Abu Dhabi, I’ve met candidates from all corners of the world. But there’s one thing that never fails to surprise me: the difference in how candidates from the West and those from Asia or Africa respond when asked a simple question—

“What are your salary expectations?”

Western candidates rarely flinch. With confidence, they state their current or previous salary, followed by a figure they believe they deserve — usually a notch or two higher. There’s no hesitation. It’s business. It’s negotiation.

But when I pose the same question to others — especially candidates from the Indian subcontinent, the Philippines, or certain African nations — the tone shifts entirely.

“Anything, Sir.”
“Whatever the company standard is.”
“I just need a job to utilize my skills, Sir.”

These aren’t just words. These are windows into lives shaped by different economies, different systems, and different levels of bargaining power.

It’s not that one group is more ambitious than the other — it’s that one is more empowered to ask.

The Western candidate has likely had a history of structured labor laws, transparent pay scales, and perhaps even a culture that encourages negotiation. The others? Often, they carry years of experience in systems where jobs are scarce, visas are golden tickets, and “company standards” — no matter how vague — are better than the uncertainty of unemployment.

What strikes me most isn't the answers themselves, but the resignation and humility in some voices. A quiet plea wrapped in politeness. A hope that their skills will matter more than their background. A belief that someone — anyone — will see their worth without them needing to name a price.

It’s not wrong. It’s not right. It’s reality.

And it makes me ask: in a land built by dreamers and doers, how do we ensure every voice, confident or cautious, gets heard fairly?
