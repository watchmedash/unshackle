The Days I Died

Note: The image is AI-generated and may not accurately reflect the people or setting in the story.


This isn't your usual horror story. It’s not fiction. It’s not fantasy. It’s real. And I lived it.

I hope, if you’re reading this, you’ll find more than just sadness here — I hope you find proof that survival is possible.

Eleven.
I'm in a Walmart bathroom, unconscious. My sister’s number is written on my forearm — so someone knows who to call to claim my body. My heart keeps stopping from the overdose, and people are yelling and pushing and trying to keep me here. I don’t want to be here. I’m nineteen. And I’m homeless.

Ten.
I walk quickly toward the bridge, sirens wailing behind me. As I step up to jump, a hand grabs my leg and slams me into the concrete. I black out. I wake up in the psych ward again. I’m eighteen. Still homeless.

Nine.
Blood pours from my wrists. It’s pretty. It’s warm. I stare as it trickles from my hands like paint. I’m fifteen. Hiding from my mother. Trying to disappear.

Eight.
A bathroom robe tie tightens around my throat. I swing in the air, hoping this will be it. But it snaps, and I hit the floor, my ankle cracking as I land. I’m thirteen. And I’m alone.

Seven.
I swallowed too much Tylenol. It hurts. I don’t want to die anymore. I just wanted the pain to stop. I'm twelve, and my mom is nowhere. No one is helping.

Six.
I raided my mother’s pill cabinet. Took everything I could. I thought I was dead already. I hallucinate. I scream. She locks me in my room to "sleep it off." I’m eleven.

Five.
A friend at school said cutting helped. I tried it. It didn't help. It hurt. A lot. I’m bleeding. I feel dizzy. I just wanted some relief. I’m ten.

Four.
I climbed onto the roof and jumped. I wanted it to end. I wanted the men to stop. I wanted my sister to love me. I wanted my mom to say "I love you." She doesn't anymore. I’m eight.

Three.
I held my breath underwater in my aunt’s pool, hidden beneath the ladder. The world grew silent and black. My sister pulled me out, scolding me for being stupid. I wish she hadn’t. I’m seven.

Two.
I stopped eating. Mommy said I was a burden. I thought maybe if I disappeared slowly, she’d be happy. I’m six.

One.
The first time I wanted to die, I was five. The night I was raped for the first time. I stood in the street and begged the universe to run me over. A car stopped. I cried that it should’ve kept going. I just wanted the pain to end.

Please, never assume someone’s story is simple.

Some of us carry darkness that even horror writers couldn’t imagine. But I survived. I now live with eleven tally marks tattooed over my scars — a reminder to never add another. A symbol of my choice to keep going.

I built a life. I built a family. I raise my child with love I never knew. And I show up, every day, for the person I used to be.

To anyone who feels broken: You are not alone. You are not too far gone. And this world still needs you in it.

Keep moving forward.
